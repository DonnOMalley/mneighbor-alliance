# MNeighbor Alliance (MNA) — Programs Playbook
## Signature Events That Give the Neighborhood a Rhythm

The [Event Playbook](EVENT_PLAYBOOK.md) covers the mechanics of executing a permitted street closure. This document is different — it defines a menu of **named, recurring community programs** that Block Leaders and Links can adopt to keep the neighborhood active year-round.

Each program below is a ready-to-run format. Pick the ones that fit your Link, customize the details, and plug them into the [Year-Round Block Engagement Calendar](BLOCK_NETWORK.md#4-year-round-block-engagement-calendar).

---

## 1. Concert Series

A recurring series of free outdoor evening concerts held at a consistent neighborhood location throughout the summer. This is the anchor social event of the warm months — low effort to attend, high visibility, and a reason for neighbors to show up repeatedly.

### Format

| Element | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cadence** | 3-5 dates spread across June through September (e.g., one per month or bi-weekly in July/August). |
| **Time** | Evening, typically 6:00 PM – 8:00 PM. Early enough for families, done before noise ordinance curfew (10:00 PM). |
| **Location** | A consistent anchor spot: a park, a tot lot, a wide boulevard, or a permitted street closure zone. Consistency builds habit — people learn "it's concert night at the park." |
| **Cost to Attend** | Free. No tickets, no cover, no sign-up. |

### Logistics Checklist

- [ ] **Book the Acts:** Recruit local or amateur musicians. Reach out to neighborhood bands, school music programs, or solo acoustic performers. Many will play for free or for a small community chest honorarium ($50-$100).
- [ ] **Power Supply:** Secure an auxiliary electrical outlet from an adjacent homeowner or use a portable generator. Run heavy-duty, outdoor-rated extension cords to the performance area.
- [ ] **The Cord Shield:** Every cord crossing a walking path must be covered by rubber industrial cord ramps or taped down with gaffer tape. This is mandatory — see the [Event Playbook](EVENT_PLAYBOOK.md) for the full safety protocol.
- [ ] **Sound Management:** Position amplifiers facing away from non-participating houses. Keep volume conversational between sets. All amplified music ceases by 10:00 PM per city noise ordinance.
- [ ] **Food Trucks (Optional):** Invite 1-2 local food trucks to park adjacent to the venue. No cost to the Link — trucks operate independently. Coordinate arrival/departure times.
- [ ] **Seating:** Attendees bring their own lawn chairs and blankets. The Link can set out a few communal folding chairs for elderly neighbors or newcomers.
- [ ] **Promotion:** Block Leaders drop flyers 1 week before each date. Post to the Link's Signal group. Add to the MNA community calendar.

### Permitting Notes
*   If the concert is held in a **public park**, check with Saint Paul Parks & Recreation for any reservation or amplified sound permit requirements.
*   If the concert requires a **street closure**, follow the full [Event Playbook](EVENT_PLAYBOOK.md) permitting sequence (45-day lead time).
*   If the concert is held on a **private yard or porch** with no street closure, no city permit is required — but the noise ordinance still applies.

## 2. Ice Cream Social

An annual mid-summer community gathering built around a simple draw: free (or suggested-donation) ice cream, kids' activities, and live music. This is the neighborhood's signature family event — maximum inclusivity, minimal barrier to attendance.

### Format

| Element | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cadence** | Once per year, mid-summer (late June or July). |
| **Time** | Afternoon, typically 1:00 PM – 3:00 PM or 2:00 PM – 4:00 PM. Daytime keeps it family-friendly and avoids sound curfew concerns. |
| **Location** | A park, tot lot, or permitted street closure zone with shade. Proximity to a playground is ideal for keeping kids engaged. |
| **Cost to Attend** | Free, with a suggested donation jar (e.g., $5/adult, $3/child) to replenish the community chest. Donations are never required. |

### Activity Stations

Set up distinct stations so families can move between them at their own pace:

| Station | What You Need | Volunteers Needed |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ice Cream Table** | Bulk ice cream tubs (3-4 flavors), cones, cups, scoop, napkins, cooler with ice. | 1-2 scoopers |
| **Live Music / Community Band** | See Concert Series logistics above. Acoustic sets work well for afternoon volume. | 1 sound coordinator |
| **Face Painting** | Washable face paint kit, brushes, mirror, wet wipes. | 1 painter (recruit a teen or art-inclined neighbor) |
| **Kids' Crafts** | Coloring sheets, markers, button-making press, stickers. Set up on a low folding table. | 1 table monitor |
| **Lawn Games** | Water balloon toss, bingo cards, sack races, cornhole boards. Use what neighbors already own. | 1 game runner |

### Budget Estimate

| Item | Estimated Cost | Funded By |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Bulk ice cream (4-5 gallons) | $40 – $60 | Community chest |
| Cones, cups, napkins | $15 – $20 | Community chest |
| Face paint kit | $10 – $15 | Community chest or donation |
| Craft supplies | $10 – $15 | Community chest or donation |
| **Total** | **$75 – $110** | |

> Suggested donations at the event typically cover or exceed costs, making this close to self-funding.

### Promotion
*   Block Leaders drop flyers 2 weeks out.
*   Post to Signal group and community calendar.
*   Use the [Club Interest Flyer Template](templates/CLUB_INTEREST_FLYER.md) as a formatting guide for the event flyer.

## 3. Movie in the Park

A free outdoor film screening on a warm evening. Simple to execute, universally appealing, and a strong draw for families and neighbors who might not attend other events.

### Format

| Element | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cadence** | 1-2 times per summer (July or August evenings work best for late sunsets). |
| **Time** | Start at dusk (typically 8:30 PM – 9:00 PM in mid-summer). Screen a film that ends before 10:30 PM to respect noise ordinance. |
| **Location** | A park, open yard, or permitted street closure zone with a flat surface and minimal ambient light. |
| **Cost to Attend** | Free. No tickets, no sign-up. |

### Equipment Checklist

| Item | Options | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Projector** | Borrow from a neighbor, school, or library. Budget portable projectors ($60-$100) work for crowds under 50. | Minimum 3,000 lumens recommended for outdoor use. |
| **Screen** | Inflatable outdoor screen, a white bedsheet hung between poles, or projected onto a light-colored wall/garage door. | A taut sheet works fine — don't overthink this. |
| **Audio** | Portable Bluetooth speaker or PA system connected to the projector's audio output. | Position facing the audience, not neighboring houses. |
| **Power** | Extension cord from an adjacent home outlet or a portable generator. | Apply the Cord Shield protocol from the Event Playbook. |
| **Seating** | Attendees bring blankets and lawn chairs. | Announce "bring your own seating" on the flyer. |

### Film Selection & Licensing

> [!WARNING]
> **Public Performance Licensing:**
> Screening a movie outdoors for a group of neighbors is technically a public performance. Studios require a public performance license for non-private screenings. Options:
> *   **Swank Motion Pictures** (swank.com) and **Criterion Pictures** offer community screening licenses, typically $150-$350 per film depending on audience size.
> *   **Free alternatives:** Some public libraries and parks departments hold blanket screening licenses and may co-sponsor your showing at no cost. Check with the Saint Paul Public Library system.
> *   **Film choice:** Family-friendly, universally appealing films draw the widest crowd. Animated films, classic comedies, and crowd-pleasers work best. Poll the Signal group for suggestions.

### Day-Of Setup Timeline

| Time | Task |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dusk minus 90 min** | Arrive. Claim the screening area. Set up screen, projector, and audio. |
| **Dusk minus 60 min** | Run a test projection to confirm image size, focus, and audio levels before the crowd arrives. |
| **Dusk minus 30 min** | Neighbors begin arriving with blankets and chairs. Popcorn station opens (optional — a neighbor with an air popper and bulk kernels). |
| **Dusk** | Start the film. |
| **Film ends** | Pack up equipment, pick up any trash, and reopen the area. |

## 4. National Night Out

**National Night Out (NNO)** is a nationwide annual event held on the **first Tuesday of August** that promotes police-community partnerships and neighborhood camaraderie. It's one of the easiest events a Block Leader can organize because the format is inherently casual and the national program provides built-in awareness.

### Format

| Element | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Date** | First Tuesday of August (fixed nationally). |
| **Time** | Evening, typically 5:00 PM – 8:00 PM. |
| **Location** | A front yard, porch, driveway, or sidewalk on the block. No street closure needed unless the block wants to go big. |
| **Cost** | Zero to minimal. This is a "whatever you've got" event. |

### The Spectrum: Minimum Viable to Full Send

NNO works at any scale. Block Leaders should pick the level that matches their block's energy:

**Level 1 — The Porch Hang (1 person can do this)**
*   Set out a cooler of drinks and a bag of chips on your front porch or lawn.
*   Text the Signal group: *"Hanging out on the porch for National Night Out tonight. Come say hi."*
*   That's it. Neighbors drift by, conversations happen.

**Level 2 — The Block Potluck (2-3 people coordinating)**
*   One neighbor hosts the gathering point (yard or driveway).
*   Post a sign-up in the Signal group: main dish, sides, drinks, dessert.
*   Set up a folding table for food. Add lawn chairs.
*   Optional: invite the kids to ride bikes or draw with sidewalk chalk.

**Level 3 — The Full Block Party (Full stewardship team engaged)**
*   Execute a permitted street closure through the [Event Playbook](EVENT_PLAYBOOK.md).
*   Add structured activities: bounce house, live music, grilling station, kids' games.
*   Invite SPPD community officers to stop by (they actively participate in NNO — contact the Western District at 651-266-5512 to request a visit).
*   Register your event at **natw.org** to be included in the national map.

### City Registration & Police Visits
*   **Saint Paul registration:** The City of Saint Paul typically promotes NNO participation through its website and district councils. Check **stpaul.gov** in late June/July for registration links.
*   **SPPD visits:** The Western District dispatches officers and sometimes fire trucks to registered NNO events. Register early — popular time slots fill up.
*   **No permit needed** for Level 1 or Level 2 gatherings on private property or sidewalks. A street closure (Level 3) requires the full Event Playbook permitting process with a 45-day lead time.

### Promotion
*   Block Leaders announce the date in the Signal group at least 2 weeks out.
*   Drop a porch flyer 1 week before with the time, location, and what to bring.
*   Day-of reminder in the Signal group that morning.

## Proposing New Programs

The four programs above are starting templates, not a closed list. Any neighbor or Block Leader can propose a new recurring program by:

1.  **Drafting a one-page format sheet** following the structure above (format table, logistics checklist, budget estimate, permitting notes).
2.  **Testing it once** as a one-off event on their block.
3.  **Sharing the results** with the Router, who can distribute it to other Block Leaders and the Mesh Council for adoption across Links.

If a new program format proves repeatable, it gets added to this playbook.
